Making Mathematics Movies

I would recommend these movies to anyone, depending on their tastes. They are my favorite movies*.

Every movie on this list is:

  • Considered great by many people. One is dubious, but movies are subjective, so screw that. The rest are objectively and well-agreed to be any definition of the word 'great.' Basically, none of these movies will be too unpopular.
  • Has caused some form of long-lasting (i.e. over a month at least) obsession in yours truly. Every movie here has permanently (so far in my admittedly short life) either altered my behavior, morals, or even psyche. I thought about putting Halloween or Jaws or Brewster McCloud or Nashville in here, but it is untrue to put them next to movies that affected me as much as Battle Royale.
  • Entertaining. Each one is so effective that the appreciation of the skill involved surpasses any negative emotions it seeks to evoke. McCabe & Mrs. Miller is indeed a sad movie, but it is so effectively told that the poetry in the story stirs up a strange warmth instead of depression. The Texas Chainsaw Massacre is truly horrifying, but the artistry on every level shifts one's focus from being horrified to being energetically impressed. (Don't believe me on that last point? Listen to this by Mark Kermode)
  • A portal to another world. Within each world awaits a rollercoaster, fireworks show, romantic weekend, acid trip, refreshing workout, haunted house extravaganza, adventurous treasure hunt, tear-jerking opera, meditative hot spring, magic show, balloon through a nitrous oxide atmosphere, tightrope walk over lava, or blissful dinner with family and friends.
  • Unforgettable, at least to me. The images and sounds have stayed embedded in my brain with no sign of going stale.
  • To be seen with an open mind.

    • Every movie on this list is not:

    • All of the aspects of item four on the list above. The virtuoso assaults on my senses from Mad Max: Fury Road or City of God are so brilliant I get excited just thinking about them, but they lack the peaceful yet somehow bold realizations found in Cléo from 5 to 7. Suspiria cured me of a recurring nightmare, but Harry Potter and the Goblet of Fire gave me many nightmares when I first saw it.
    • Flawless. To be flawless is not the same as great. The Amazing Spider-Man has many flaws (it is the one I mentioned earlier that people would disagree is great), but its palpable emotions come crashing over me in ways that Vertigo never could. However, some movies in this list, in fact many, I would argue are flawless. But, as stated, this is beside the point.
    • For everyone. The easily saddened should not see City of God. The squeamish should not see No Country for Old Men. Conservative catholics probably would not enjoy Superbad.
    • In any particular order.
