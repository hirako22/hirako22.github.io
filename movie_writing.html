Why I don't like Romance in Movies

Alec Lau

I just finished watching Like Water for Chocolate (1992), and, while I thought it was a good movie, it wasn't my cup of tea, or rather pozole. The movie reminded me of why I find most movies very lacking in terms of romance, although this isn't the movie's fault. Rather, the premise of this Romeo and Juliet story is my problem with romance movies in general. The premise: Pedro and Tita make eye contact a few times, and then profess their love for each other. Their undying love for each other fuels all of the events in the movie, except wait a minute; why are they so in love with each other? The only reasoning the movie seems to give is that they are the most attractive characters. I acknowledge that this doesn't matter, that the correct thing to do is say "but okay, they love each other," that it wouldn't be practical for them to know each other given the cartoonishly evil Mama Elena. But I think this highlights the flaws in less capricious, more pretentious movies.

I admit, I used to think my aversion to watching romance on the big screen is just some residual immaturity from my days as a sweaty, cars-and-trucks-loving, blow-something-up young boy. The few romances I had seen in movies for most of my childhood consist of: Anakin and Padme, Bella and Edward, the two leads in Top Gun so uninteresting and unmemorable that I can't recall their names, Aang and Katara, Neo and Trinity, and that's all I can remember. Their weakness must have been why I have a pavlovian cringe and/or roll my eyes whenever a romance is shown on screen. Except I still find almost every movie romance I watch pretty disengaging. The above romances are pretty understandable; what about movies where the relationship is the focus, and the characters are more than 0-dimensional? All the ones I've seen still take on the following form: they meet, and they're in love. But this is not actually love - it's lust. I sympathize; it's tough to form a romance in a non-instant way when you've got other stuff going on. That's fine, but don't try to shove the message that 'they have undying love for each other' down my throat. For almost every movie romance, the movie resorts to a 'love at first sight' excuse for giving us the premise that the two characters are in love, even if the movie is about them being in love. Can't we evolve past this lazy development? Even Shakespeare, in Romeo and Juliet, had Romeo 'fall in love' with another character, before switching it up with Juliet around fifteen minutes later. He further made fun of this in A Midsummer Nights' Dream.

Notice I used the word 'almost.' I now list the movie romances I had no problem with, for various reasons. The first is this: it's okay to be lazy with the premise of a romance, if they movie isn't about said romance. A good example is Mulholland Drive (2001). A lot of this plot hinges on the romance, but the movie makes clear that a) this is lust, and b) it doesn't even matter. What about romances where the whole movie is about developing said romance? The few I don't have a problem with are Annie Hall (1977), Heartthrob (2017), (500) Days of Summer (1977), The Amazing Spider-Man (I & II) (2012/2014), Roman Holiday (1977), where these movies try to sell the audience that yes, this is indeed love, and not lust as a way to convey a theme. Let's take them one by one.

Annie Hall doesn't try to convince us at all that they have undying love for each other. We see the relationship start slowly. They like the way the other person looks (in most other movies this would be enough). They share the same interests and are interested in what the other person does. Then it falls apart, but we get to know the characters and find that, yes, they are pretty compatible, and yes, it makes sense they would care about one another. However, the movie ends up acknowledging that this wasn't love, they were just very fond of each other, and still are. By making their relationship this realistic makes us all the more invested in these two peoples' relationship.

Heartthrob isn't about love, although the characters think they are in love with each other. The entire movie is a tragedy surrounding this misconception. When one side believes they're in love with the other, it is because of mental illness: obsessiveness coupled with a toxic relationship with a domineering parent. As for the other side, they mistake promiscuity as self-worth due to an event that often results in this behavior, and the misconception that a partner seeing through this means they are truly loved; in other words, trauma and connection issues. Throw in typical emotions in a teen and one has a recipe for mistaking strong anger, frustration, depression, etc. for love. This is an interesting idea, except why do we get invested in it? For one, the characters are relatable and the dialogue is quite realistic. Second, we can see how their personalities are compatible, and thirdly, we want to distract ourselves from the clouds on the horizon. We care about the relationship because we care about the characters, not because they're in 'love.'

When is a Movie Good? An Axiomatic Approach

Alec Lau

Here I attempt to classify a large amount of definitions and heuristics about what one means when referring to a movie as 'good' or 'great.' I will discuss more specific properties that can follow, their intersections, and, more interestingly, their complements. With each step of the way I will examine the role of subjectivity, taken itself as an axiom. I will attempt to provide the largest amount of examples for each case that does not cloud the structure of this writing.

To avoid a useless semantic discussion on 'what it means to be good (great)', I have compiled a list of properties a good movie should have and what a great movie should have. This is based off of a heuristic but nonetheless intuitive approach that should hopefully encompass most of what is commonly meant by 'good' and 'great.' Note that this is not a proposal of new definitions for the words 'good' and 'great,' but rather classifications that encompass properties that determine whether a movie is 'good' or 'great.'
