The Top 11 Best Avatar: the Last Airbender Episodes

Avatar: the Last Airbender (henceforth referred to as 'Avatar'; screw James Cameron's unoriginal, already dated-looking smurf movie) is, in my opinion, the greated show of all time. This is of course limited to what shows I have seen, but this show blows all other shows I've seen away. It is filled with beautiful stories, in-depth messages, unforgettable characters, amazing visuals, enlightening philosophy, and sparkling emotion, all against a backdrop of a whirlwind plot with amazing world building. The reason I have selected 11 as a number is for the same reason I selected 11 as my movie number; not mentioning any of these episodes would be a crime, and 11 is a nice number. Simplistic characters, prime, whatever.

The Storm

The first couple of episodes of Avatar were wonderful in their own right, but it just seemed like another skillful kids' show. Then "the Winter Solstice" episodes happened, and we all could tell we were in for a truly fun ride. There was actual palpable, adult pressure. Aang has work to do. The fire nation is already moving fast, and a day approaches when they will be unstoppable. There is no one else who can do this. Into the warzone we go.

We knew we had an adventure on our hands, and, given the scenery and creativity of bending, it was going to be both interesting and fun. However, we didn't know just how powerful the storytelling was going to get, nor how deep the characters were, nor how many emotions were about to come thrashing out of the flood gates until this episode.

Almost nothing happens in this episode. A storm comes, Sokka and a fisherman are threatened, Aang saves them. Zuko's crew is threatened, and Zuko eventually lets them rest after leading them to safety. But this episode tells us all we need to know, and suggests a bit more. In alternating campfire scenes, we learn the backstories of Aang and Zuko. Aang learns he is the Avatar, and we feel the full pressure on him. Two of his mentors fight about how to raise him. The monk closest to him doesn't want to rob him of his childhood. The other sees clouds forming from the fire nation, and takes the utilitarian position; the world needs the avatar now, and the sacrifice of a boy's humanity is a necessary toll to keep the world in balance. Aang overhears this, and runs away. When he wakes up, the world is on the brink of shambles. We understand that, hidden behind his childlike whimsy lies a tremendous guilt.

Zuko is a young prince, ready to make his father proud. In a demonstration of something not quite evil, he is punished by his ruthless and power-hungry father. Because of this, we see again a display of Zuko's not-so-evil principles, and he is banished, and here we are. We understand that Zuko bottles his conflicting shame and messed-up familial relationships into catching the avatar, and we wonder if Zuko is truly bad.

The storm in the episode is clearly a metaphor for the coming storm in the world of avatar. While Aang turned his back on the world before, he is not turning back any more, and will save those in need from now on. Zuko learns to put away his inner demons (for a moment at least) and does what is right for the people around him. It would have been fine to just introduce the backstories of the characters separately; one would get the same depth regardless. But this makers of this show understand that some of the best villains are the ones most similar to the protagonists, giving a backstory yin and yang, itself in line with the show's many Daoist themes.

I think it is probably true that this is the episode when people realize that Avatar isn't simply another show. It is when people realize that the makers of this show, once they prove the concept to the executives, are going to take this show as deep as they want with an enormous amount of skill.

Tales of Ba Sing Se

Here is another episode in which nothing happens. We simply observe everyday events, and appreciate simplicity, a key principle of Daoism. It is almost a form of meditation where we simply sit with the characters we have come to know and love. Toph and Katara, two differing characters who have hit heads in the past improve on their relationship. (not that this lasted more than an episode, thank goodness. Aren't bickering main characters just the worst?)Toph shows a vulnerable side, making us love her even more, and, for a cherry on top, mean girls get what's coming to them.

Iroh's story speaks for itself. If you haven't seen this episode, watch the entire show, but also suffice it to say that the number one instance where people cry during this show is during Iroh's story.

In The Tale of Aang, he makes a zoo, freeing animals from the dismal original zoo. Appa is still missing in this episode, and through this tale we still feel Aang's yearning for his oldest surviving friend. The danger of the whole Appa debacle was that Aang would be back to normal after an episode of his mourning. The creators wisely stray away from this; they have too much respect for themselves and the audience. This adds more realism and heartache in an already heartbreaking season. In my calculation, 13/20 episodes in this season end on sad notes. That's just plain badass, especially for a kids' show. I recall, when I was a kid, associating this show with a merciless but irresistible sadness that challenged me in ways I had never been challenged before. But I digress. The imagery of Aang and the voice acting is to be commended. It takes a good actor to be a good actor, but a great actor to be a bad actor. By which I mean we can hear Aang trying to sound happy, but hints of deep loss leak through.

Sokka is Zulu for "lover boy." How fitting for him to stumble into a girls' group of poetry, and it is a testament to the creativity of the writers to have a haiku battle take place. In usual (but nowhere near constant) Sokka fashion, his story provides relaxation from the intense emotions in the rest of the characters.

We rarely see Zuko's humanity, and even less so him being a normal teenage boy. In his tale, he goes out on a date. He is reluctant at first, as the many hardships he's faced have made his shell very thick. Here we also see on of the first instances of firebending being portrayed in a good light. The culture of fire is still protrayed by the show to be a part of this world, and not just a menace. The forms, dances, culture, and fashion are given proper respect; there is no "the bad guys are evil and ugly" simplifying here, as opposed to stories like The Lord of the Rings. Zuko here learns to let go of his shame. Granted, this is the shame of being a firebender and not being banished and labeled a national disgrace by all he held dear, but it is invigorating to see Zuko come out of his shell just once. Around this time, we come to like Zuko quite a bit for a villain.

In The Tale of Momo, we get a story without words, but emotion is still present. We are shown that Appa's disappearance has affected the entire group, and that Aang has just been the focus. In typical Avatar fashion, we end on a sad note but also a "wait a second!" moment: the last shot is of Momo cuddling himself inside Appa's footprint, alone, reminding us of Appa's absence. The sky is dark, and it is raining a melancholy rain. But Wait A Second! Appa was here!
